Springtelling — The Gentle Face

This is the story the elders tell when the frost breaks, when the first green spears push through last year’s leaf‑rot, and the air smells like wet earth and promise.

Before humans named the trees, before they built the roads, there was the Root Mother. She was the deep pulse of the forest, her hair spilling into soil, her voice a tremor in the roots.

Every seed knew her touch. Every sprout knew her breath.

She did not command; she nourished. Roots whispered secrets to her, and she kept them safe. If you were lost, she wrapped you in green and showed the way home. If you were hungry, she gave you berries and tubers, but always with the promise you would plant something in return.

Law of Spring: Nothing grows alone.

And so the people learned to tend the earth as they would a child, and the forest grew thick around them like kin.

Autumntelling — The Dark Face

This one is told when the leaves turn red, when the wind smells of smoke, and the soil feels restless underfoot.

Long after the covenant was made, some forgot her law. They raised walls the roots could not pierce, paved the rivers, stripped the hills, and laughed when the ground cracked beneath them.

The Root Mother waited. She always waits.

Her roots rose in the night, silent as breath. They wormed through stone foundations, split cobbles, swallowed beams.

The first house fell without a sound—its stones already tangled in a net of roots that pulled it under like a fish caught in a dark, patient sea.

Then another. And another.

Some say they saw her—a vast woman‑shaped shadow, hair like a canopy of leaves, eyes black as fresh soil, smiling with the stillness of someone who has all the time in the world.

When the cities were gone, the hollows filled with forest again. Birdsong returned. Mushrooms bloomed where market stalls had once stood.

Law of Autumn: The earth remembers. The earth takes back.

The Cycle

In spring, they tell her gentle face—so children will learn the joy of planting, and strangers will feel welcome in the forest.

In autumn, they tell her dark face—so no one forgets what happens when you break the covenant.

And in winter, they speak neither name aloud. For winter is the time she sleeps deepest, and they will not wake her before the thaw.
